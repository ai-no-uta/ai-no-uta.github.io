Rounding with Floor and Ceiling Functions

The floor and ceiling functions give ways to round to the largest/smallest integer less than/greater than a number \(x\), respectively (sorry for the phrasing of this sentence). Their use can be extended, however, to finding the largest/smallest multiple of any number of the form \(\frac{1}{n}\), where \(n\) is a natural number greater than \(0\), less than/greater than a number.

The following describes the method for just the floor function, as the method for the ceiling function is analogous.

Let \(x\) be the input number. Suppose that we want to find the largest multiple \(k\) of a positive number \(a\) such that \(ka ≤ x\).

Equivalently, we wish to find the greatest \(k\) that is less than or equal to \(\frac{x}{a}\). Set \(k\) to equal \(\lfloor \frac{x}{a}\rfloor\). Then, our desired multiple is equal to \(\lfloor \frac{x}{a}\rfloor a\).

We can check that this works: \(\lfloor \frac{x}{a}\rfloor a ≤ \frac{x}{a}a = x\), so this multiple is indeed less than or equal to our original number, and moreover \(\lfloor \frac{x}{a}+1\rfloor a > \frac{x}{a}a = x\), so defining \(k\) as we did gave us the largest such multiple.

To find the least multiple of \(a\) greater than or equal to \(x\) instead, I suggest you use the ceiling function. If \(a\) is negative, I believe that you will need to phrase the question differently (there is no greatest multiple of \(a\) less than or equal to \(x\), but there is a least one) and then to swap the use of floor and ceiling functions due to the inequality flipping when dividing by a negative number, but the overall process should work out.
