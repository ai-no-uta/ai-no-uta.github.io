French Conjugations

Introduction

Conjugations in French are pretty nasty. I think that we can be in agreement about that. And it's sort of salt in the wound that they are often hard or impossible to distinguish in speech. You spend all this time and effort and for what payoff? So that French people don't sneer at your illiteracy when you write to them? Ugh. Anyways, I want to examine some of the French conjugations: how they are formed, how many conjugation classes there are, and hopefully also their historical development. I hope that you are as excited to investigate the story of how French conjugation got to be this weird as I am. At some point in time, I will also try to pull a list of all the verbs belonging to each paradigm so that we can all gawk at some statistics together. I love numbers! We live in the era of numbers! Quantify everything, even if that means you are distorting or losing important information. Muahaha. But in this case I think numbers will actually be helpful.

Present Indicative

There are 3-4 regular conjugation classes in the present indicative, depending on your tastes. I will maintain for the time being that there are four until the coherence of one (or more?) of them fall apart under heavier scrutiny, or reveals itself to be too small to deserve the title of 'regular conjugation class'. They are:

  1. -er verbs
  2. -re verbs
  3. -ir verbs with ss
  4. -ir verbs without ss

-er Verbs

Attach the following to the stem of the verb, which consists of the infinitive with the -er ending removed. If the stem ends with the letters c or ge, change them to ç or ge respectively to indicate that the pronunciation does not change.

Person →
Number ↓ 		1 2 3
S -e -es -e
P -ons -ez -ent

Example:

Person →
Number ↓ 		1 2 3
S cache caches cache
P cachons cachez cachent

-re Verbs

Attach the following endings. The stem is formed as in the previous. I think that all verbs in this category have stems ending in d

Person →
Number ↓ 		1 2 3
S -s -s -∅
P -ons -ez -ent

Example:

Person →
Number ↓ 		1 2 3
S rends rends rend
P rendons rendez rendent

-ir verbs with ss

This is a very strange category of verbs, for the stem is different in the singular and plural cases. In the singular, the stem is simply the infinitive with -ir removed. In the plural, however, iss is added to the singular stem before the person/number suffixes are added.

Person →
Number ↓ 		1 2 3
S -is -is -it
P -ons -ez -ent

Example:

Person →
Number ↓ 		1 2 3
S finis finis finit
P finissons finissez finissent

-ir verbs without ss

Another quirky category. I am not sure how to distinguish verbs here from those in the preceding category by sight, yet. To form the stem for the singular conjugations, delete the -ir ending as well as the preceding consonant before attaching the endings. For the plural conjugations, delete only the -ir. This is a rather small category.

Person →
Number ↓ 		1 2 3
S -s -s -t
P -ons -ez -ent

An example:

Person →
Number ↓ 		1 2 3
S dors dors dort
P dormons dormez dorment
Note that even in the singular endings, this paradigm differs from the one with ss - the i at the end of the infinitive simply does not show up!

Imperfect

The imperfect is regular for all verbs except être. It is derived from the 1P stem, to which the following endings are attached.

Person →
Number ↓ 		1 2 3
S -ais -ais -ait
P -ions -iez -aient

Present Subjunctive

The present subjunctive is formed by taking the present 3P present indicative stem and attaching the following endings:

Person →
Number ↓ 		1 2 3
S -e -es -e
P -ions -iez -ent
This means that under regular circumstances, the forms may look like the indicative in the 1S, 2P, 3S, and 3P, and the imperfect in the 1P and 2P.

Examples:

Person →
Number ↓ 		1 2 3
S parle parles parle
P parlions parliez parlent
Person →
Number ↓ 		1 2 3
S rende rendes rende
P rendions rendiez rendent
Person →
Number ↓ 		1 2 3
S finisse finisses finisse
P finissions finissiez finissent
Person →
Number ↓ 		1 2 3
S dorme dormes dorme
P dormions dormiez dorment

Note that as the 3P stem may differ from the 1P stem, as the following example demonstrates:

Person →
Number ↓ 		1 2 3
S boive boives boive
P boivions boiviez boivent
So it makes sense to emphasize third person stem after all!

However, there are certain verbs for which this pattern doesn't hold: the present indicative 3P form of faire is font, for instance - surely we aren't putting those endings directly onto the letter f? Indeed that is not the case.

Person →
Number ↓ 		1 2 3
S fasse fasses fasse
P fassions fassiez fassent
