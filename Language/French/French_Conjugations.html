French Conjugations

Introduction

Conjugations in French are pretty nasty. I think that we can be in agreement about that. And it's sort of salt in the wound that they are often hard or impossible to distinguish in speech. You spend all this time and effort and for what payoff? So that French people don't sneer at your illiteracy when you write to them? Ugh. Anyways, I want to examine some of the French conjugations: how they are formed, how many conjugation classes there are, and hopefully also their historical development. I hope that you are as excited to investigate the story of how French conjugation got to be this weird as I am.

My current dataset comprises a whopping 5361 verbs. Note that there are some duplicates due to different conventions in spelling. I will heuristically (for no significant reason) split the verbs into four classes.

  • Verbs ending with -er: 4706
  • Verbs ending with -re: 252
  • Verbs ending with either -ire or -oir: 41
  • Verbs ending with -ir: 362
There are clear imbalances in the sizes of the classes. More than 87% of verbs end with -er! This is wonderful, especially considering that this class will prove to be the most regular. However, this does not mean, unfortunately, that you can neglect the other three, as verbs in those will prove to be outsizedly common. Curses!

Present Indicative

-er Verbs

Attach the following to the stem of the verb, which consists of the infinitive with the -er ending removed.

Person →
Number ↓ 		1 2 3
S -e -es -e
P -ons -ez -ent

Example:

Person →
Number ↓ 		1 2 3
S cache caches cache
P cachons cachez cachent

There are 4098 verbs (out of 4706) that lack any irregularity whatsoever. This is more than 87% again. This is a handsome amount, although it leaves a disappointingly large number of irregular verbs in the class as well. Luckily, the irregularities here are not two significant. Some are only orthographical, even.

For instance, if the verb's stem ends with the letters c or g, then they become ç or ge, respectively, in 1P to indicate that the pronunciation remains soft.

Example:

Person →
Number ↓ 		1 2 3
S mange manges mange
P mangeons mangez mangent

If the stem ends with y, it will change to i in the singular and 3P conjugations in most cases. I think the exception is when the stem ends with ay.

Example:

Person →
Number ↓ 		1 2 3
S appuie appuies appuie
P appuyons appuyez appuient

There is also sometimes a stem vowel alternation. If the letter e appears in the last syllable of the stem, this vowel may become è in the singular and 3P conjugations instead.

Example:

Person →
Number ↓ 		1 2 3
S achète achètes achète
P achetons achetez achètent

Sometimes, this vowel alternation is conveyed through the doubling of the final consonant of the stem, instead. This occurs regularly(?) if the last consonant is l, but only occasionally if it is t.

Example:

Person →
Number ↓ 		1 2 3
S appelle appelles appelle
P appelons appelez appellent

Verbs ending with -oir/-oire

Very irregular. Learn on a case by case basis. Sorry!

-re Verbs

Oh hell no! Oh hell no! Oh hell no! I understand now why French grammarians don't recognize verbs in this class as regular. I identify three significant patterns. Form the stem by removing the ending -re. Then, the regular patterns are:

  • Stem ending in d: 43
  • Stem ending in ît: 36
  • Stem ending in i: 42
These three together amount to a measly 121 out of the 252 - less than half! How pathetic! Let us go through them.

For verbs with stems ending in d that are regular, attach the following endings to the stem:

Person →
Number ↓ 		1 2 3
S -s -s -∅
P -ons -ez -ent

Example:

Person →
Number ↓ 		1 2 3
S rends rends rend
P rendons rendez rendent

For verbs with stems ending in ît, remove the final t and drop the circumflex for all conjugations except 3S and add the following endings:

Person →
Number ↓ 		1 2 3
S -s -s -t
P -ssons -ssez -ssent

Example:

Person →
Number ↓ 		1 2 3
S connais connais connaît
P connaissons connaissez connaissent

For verbs with stems ending in i, an ending pattern I have noticed is:

Person →
Number ↓ 		1 2 3
S -s -s -t
P -sons -sez -sent

Example:

Person →
Number ↓ 		1 2 3
S conduis conduis conduit
P conduisons conduisez conduisent

For a small minority of verbs (just 10), the following endings are used:

Person →
Number ↓ 		1 2 3
S -s -s -t
P ons ez -ent

Example:

Person →
Number ↓ 		1 2 3
S ris ris rit
P rions riez rient

There are 28 verbs with stem ending in -ind where the nd becomes gn in the plural conjugations. In the singular, remove the final -d from the stem instead and attach the following:

Person →
Number ↓ 		1 2 3
S -s -s -t
P ons ez -ent

Example:

Person →
Number ↓ 		1 2 3
S crains crains craint
P craignons craignez craignent

For some verbs rhyming with mettre, the singular stems have a single t. Attach the following endings:

Person →
Number ↓ 		1 2 3
S -s -s -∅
P ons ez -ent

Example:

Person →
Number ↓ 		1 2 3
S mets mets met
P mettons mettez mettent

For some verbs with stem ending with a closing diphthong expressing i, it becomes y in 1P and 2P. The exceptional ending for stems not ending with d,t is used.

Example:

Person →
Number ↓ 		1 2 3
S brais brais brait
P brayons brayez braient

Person →

-ir verbs without ss

Another quirky category, numbering something fewer than 81. The following endings are used, but there are frequently irregularities in the stem. Sometimes, the final consonant of the stem is dropped in the singular, like in dormir or servir.

Person →
Number ↓ 		1 2 3
S -s -s -t
P -ons -ez -ent

An example:

Person →
Number ↓ 		1 2 3
S dors dors dort
P dormons dormez dorment
Note that even in the singular endings, this paradigm differs from the one with ss - the i at the end of the infinitive simply does not show up!

Present Subjunctive

The present subjunctive is formed by taking the present 3P present indicative stem and attaching the following endings:

Person →
Number ↓ 		1 2 3
S -e -es -e
P -ions -iez -ent
This means that under regular circumstances, the forms may look like the indicative in the 1S, 2P, 3S, and 3P, and the imperfect in the 1P and 2P.

Examples:

Person →
Number ↓ 		1 2 3
S parle parles parle
P parlions parliez parlent
Person →
Number ↓ 		1 2 3
S rende rendes rende
P rendions rendiez rendent
Person →
Number ↓ 		1 2 3
S finisse finisses finisse
P finissions finissiez finissent
Person →
Number ↓ 		1 2 3
S dorme dormes dorme
P dormions dormiez dorment

Note that as the 3P stem may differ from the 1P stem, as the following example demonstrates:

Person →
Number ↓ 		1 2 3
S boive boives boive
P boivions boiviez boivent
So it makes sense to emphasize third person stem after all!

However, there are certain verbs for which this pattern doesn't hold: the present indicative 3P form of faire is font, for instance - surely we aren't putting those endings directly onto the letter f? Indeed that is not the case.

Number ↓ 		1 2 3
S fasse fasses fasse
P fassions fassiez fassent
