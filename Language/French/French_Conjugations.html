French Conjugations

Introduction

Conjugations in French are pretty nasty. I think that we can be in agreement about that. And it's sort of salt in the wound that they are often hard or impossible to distinguish in speech. You spend all this time and effort and for what payoff? So that French people don't sneer at your illiteracy when you write to them? Ugh. Anyways, I want to examine some of the French conjugations: how they are formed, how many conjugation classes there are, and hopefully also their historical development. I hope that you are as excited to investigate the story of how French conjugation got to be this weird as I am.

My current dataset comprises a whopping 5289 verbs. Note that there are some duplicates due to different conventions in spelling. I will heuristically (for no significant reason) split the verbs into four classes.

  • Verbs ending with -er: 4657
  • Verbs ending with -ir: 359
  • Verbs ending with -re: 232
  • Verbs ending with either -ire or -oir: 41
There are clear imbalances in the sizes of the classes. More than 88% of verbs end with -er! This is wonderful, especially considering that this class will prove to be the most regular. However, this does not mean, unfortunately, that you can neglect the other three, as verbs in those will prove to be outsizedly common. Curses!

After some thought, I have decided to revise my analysis to shift some of the material from suffixes onto stems. This allows me to have a uniform description of the suffixes used in the different conjugations, at the cost of forcing the learner to memorize more stems instead. I believe that this is more parsimonious, as stems will be reused across TAMs.

Present Indicative

-er Verbs

Attach the following to the stem of the verb, which consists of the infinitive with the -er ending removed.

Person →
Number ↓ 		1 2 3
S -e -es -e
P -ons -ez -ent

Example:

Person →
Number ↓ 		1 2 3
S cache caches cache
P cachons cachez cachent

There are 4054 verbs (out of 4657) that lack any irregularity whatsoever. This is more than 87% - a handsome amount, but it leaves a disappointingly large number of irregular verbs in the class as well. Luckily, the irregularities here are not two significant. Some are only orthographical, even.

For instance, if the verb's stem ends with the letters c or g, then they become ç or ge, respectively, in 1P to indicate that the pronunciation remains soft. There are 256 of such verbs.

Example:

Person →
Number ↓ 		1 2 3
S mange manges mange
P mangeons mangez mangent

If the stem ends with y, it will change to i in the singular and 3P conjugations in most cases. I think the exception is when the stem ends with ay. There are 67 of such verbs.

Example:

Person →
Number ↓ 		1 2 3
S appuie appuies appuie
P appuyons appuyez appuient

There is also sometimes a stem vowel alternation. If the letter e appears in the last syllable of the stem, this vowel may become è in the singular and 3P conjugations instead. There are 250 such cases.

Example:

Person →
Number ↓ 		1 2 3
S achète achètes achète
P achetons achetez achètent

Sometimes, this vowel alternation is conveyed through the doubling of the final consonant of the stem, instead. This occurs regularly(?) if the last consonant is l, but only occasionally if it is t. There are 16 and 14 such cases, respectively.

Example:

Person →
Number ↓ 		1 2 3
S appelle appelles appelle
P appelons appelez appellent

-ir verbs

The regular conjugation in this class has 270 members, which comes out to 270/359 or about 75% of the verbs ending with -ir that do not end with -oir. These verbs have different singular and plural stems. For the singular, remove the final -ir. For the plural, do that, but then append -iss. Then, add the following endings:

Person →
Number ↓ 		1 2 3
S -is -is -it
P -ons -ez -ent

Example:

Person →
Number ↓ 		1 2 3
S finis finis finit
P finissons finissez finissent

19 typically common verbs comprising dormir, mentir, partir, repentir, sentir, servir, sortir and their derivatives conjugate in the same way. Again, there is a dichotomy between the singular and plural stems, but they are formed differently. The singular stem is formed by removing the infinitive ending -ir as well as the preceding consonant; the plural stem is formed just by removing -ir.

Person →
Number ↓ 		1 2 3
S -s -s -t
P -ons -ez -ent

An example:

Person →
Number ↓ 		1 2 3
S dors dors dort
P dormons dormez dorment

15 verbs, including couvrir, cueillir, offrir, ouvrir and their derivatives form another miniature pattern. Form the stem by removing -ir and conjugate as if you had an -er verb.

Person →
Number ↓ 		1 2 3
S ouvre ouvres ouvre
P ouvrons ouvrez ouvrent

28 verbs rhyming with tenir, venir form another class. There is a stem alternation, but, in contrast to two of the previous patterns, this is between the 1P and 2P on one hand and everything else on the other. The 1P/2P stem is formed just by removing -ir, but for the others, the e in the preceding syllable also changes to ie. In the 3P, the final n of the stem is also duplicated. Then, the dormir class endings are added.

Person →
Number ↓ 		1 2 3
S viens viens vient
P venons venez viennent

-re Verbs

Oh hell no! Oh hell no! Oh hell no! I understand now why French grammarians don't recognize verbs in this class as regular. I identify the following significant patterns:

  • 'Regular enough' -re verbs: 17
  • Infinitives ending in -dre, like mordre: 42
  • Infinitives ending in -ître, like paraître: 16 (+1)
  • Infinitives ending in -aindre, like craindre: 28
  • Infinitives ending in -ttre, like mettre: 29
  • Conjugations patterning like cuire: 49 (+1)
These together amount to at least 181/231, or more than 78 percent of the verbs ending in -re.

There are a measly 17 verbs that are essentially regular. Form the stem by lopping off the final -re. In 7 of them, namely braire and derivatives of traire, the final i of the stem turns into y in the 1P and 2P forms. Add the following endings, which will hold for the rest of the -re verbs unless mentioned otherwise.

Person →
Number ↓ 		1 2 3
S -s -s -∅
P -ons -ez -ent

Example:

Person →
Number ↓ 		1 2 3
S ris ris rit
P rions riez rient

For verbs like mordre, form the stem by lopping off -re, and add the following suffixes. The only difference from the regular suffixes is that no -t is added in 3S.

Person →
Number ↓ 		1 2 3
S -s -s -∅
P -ons -ez -ent

Example:

Person →
Number ↓ 		1 2 3
S mords mords mord
P mordons mordez mordent

Verbs like paraître are kind of like regular -ir verbs: there is a stem alternation between the singular and plural conjugations, with the latter having an excresential ss. Form the singular stem by lopping off the final -tre from the infinitive. Form the plural stem by doing that and adding -iss. Then, add the same suffixes that you see in regular -ir verbs. Note that in all conjugations except 3S, the circumflex over the i at the end of the stem disappears. The verb that mostly fits here is recroître, which takes circumflexes in all conjugations to distinguish itself from those of recroire.

Example:

Person →
Number ↓ 		1 2 3
S connais connais connaît
P connaissons connaissez connaissent

Verbs acting like cuire typically end with -uire with the exception of circoncire, clore (obsolete), lire, plaire, suffire, taire and their derivatives, as well as most derivatives of dire (but not dire itself or redire due to an irregularity in the 2P, and not maudire for God knows why.) The singular stem is formed by lopping off the final -re, and the plural stem is formed by doing so and then adding an s. The verb that mostly fits here is plaire, which takes a circumflex over the i in 3S.

Example:

Person →
Number ↓ 		1 2 3
S conduis conduis conduit
P conduisons conduisez conduisent

There are 28 verbs with the infinitive ending in -aindre where the nd becomes gn in the plural conjugations. In the singular, remove the final -d.

Example:

Person →
Number ↓ 		1 2 3
S crains crains craint
P craignons craignez craignent

For some verbs rhyming with mettre, the singular stems have a single t. The endings are as for tne mordre pattern.

Person →
Number ↓ 		1 2 3
S -s -s -∅
P ons ez -ent

Example:

Person →
Number ↓ 		1 2 3
S mets mets met
P mettons mettez mettent

Beyond this dribble of classes, there are other verbs ending in -re that exhibit their own idiosyncrasies, such as those related to prendre, écrire, suivre, faire, etc. These just have to learned rotely.

Verbs ending with -oir/-oire

Very irregular. Learn on a case by case basis. Sorry!

The imperfect is regular for all verbs except être. It is derived from the 1P stem, to which the following endings are attached.

Present Subjunctive

The present subjunctive is formed by taking the present 3P present indicative stem and attaching the following endings:

Person →
Number ↓ 		1 2 3
S -e -es -e
P -ions -iez -ent
This means that under regular circumstances, the forms may look like the indicative in the 1S, 2P, 3S, and 3P, and the imperfect in the 1P and 2P.

Examples:

Person →
Number ↓ 		1 2 3
S parle parles parle
P parlions parliez parlent
Person →
Number ↓ 		1 2 3
S rende rendes rende
P rendions rendiez rendent
Person →
Number ↓ 		1 2 3
S finisse finisses finisse
P finissions finissiez finissent
Person →
Number ↓ 		1 2 3
S dorme dormes dorme
P dormions dormiez dorment

Note that as the 3P stem may differ from the 1P stem, as the following example demonstrates:

Person →
Number ↓ 		1 2 3
S boive boives boive
P boivions boiviez boivent
So it makes sense to emphasize third person stem after all!

However, there are certain verbs for which this pattern doesn't hold: the present indicative 3P form of faire is font, for instance - surely we aren't putting those endings directly onto the letter f? Indeed that is not the case.

Person →
Number ↓ 		1 2 3
S fasse fasses fasse
P fassions fassiez fassent
